NYUAD iGEM 2026 - WET LAB EXPERIMENTS AND WHAT THEY CALIBRATE ON THE WEBSITE
===========================================================================

What this file is
-----------------
Every model in the toolkit runs on real equations, but several numbers inside those
equations are placeholders taken from the literature. To make the website predict our
own system rather than a generic one, the wet lab has to measure those numbers and hand
them back. This file lists each experiment, the exact place on the website it feeds, how
tightly it drives the prediction (correlation), how hard it is to run (feasibility), and
how urgent it is (priority).

No result here is assumed or invented. Where a number is currently a placeholder, it is
labelled as such. Do not report any of these as measured until the experiment is actually
done.

Where the numbers live in the code
----------------------------------
All calibratable constants sit in one file: src/lib/physics/constants.ts, written as
Calib objects that carry a value, its units, a literature anchor, the experiment that
sets it, and a slider range. To update the website after an experiment, change the value
in that one file. Nothing else needs to be touched.

Priority key (out of 10)
------------------------
9 to 10  Blocks a quantitative claim. The website is only illustrative for this number
         until it is measured.
5 to 8   Important for realism. The prediction is directionally right without it but the
         magnitude may be off.
2 to 4   Refinement. Do these once the essentials are in.

Correlation key
---------------
Strong    Moving this number moves the headline output roughly one for one.
Moderate  It shifts the output noticeably but other factors share the load.
Weak      It fine tunes shape or edge behaviour, not the headline number.

Feasibility key
--------------
Easy      Standard bench work, equipment the team already has or can borrow.
Moderate  Needs a specific instrument or a multi day protocol.
Hard      Needs specialised equipment, outside facility time, or a built strain.

MODULE 1 - FLUX BALANCE ANALYSIS  (website: "Flux Balance Analysis")
===========================================================================
This module solves for how the cell splits carbon between growth and product. The two
uptake sliders and the precursor readout are the direct physical link to the bench.

EXPERIMENT 1.1 - Glucose uptake rate
  What to do: Grow the strain in a batch or fed batch bioreactor. Sample the broth for
    glucose (YSI analyser or HPLC) and for biomass (OD600 converted to dry cell weight
    using a dry weight calibration curve) over time. During exponential growth the
    specific uptake rate is (1 over biomass) times the slope of glucose against time.
  Website match: the "Glucose uptake bound" slider in the FBA module (constant vGlcMax).
  Correlation: Strong. This is the feed that the whole linear program is scaled against.
    Every downstream flux, including product and growth, moves with it.
  Feasibility: Moderate. Needs a bioreactor or well controlled flask run plus glucose
    and biomass assays.
  Priority: 9.

EXPERIMENT 1.2 - Oxygen uptake ceiling
  What to do: Measure oxygen uptake by off gas mass balance or respirometry at a known
    dry cell weight, or run a dissolved oxygen stat.
  Website match: the "Oxygen uptake bound" slider (constant vO2Max).
  Correlation: Moderate. It sets when the cell is forced into fermentation and acetate
    overflow, so it mostly matters near the low oxygen regime.
  Feasibility: Moderate. Needs off gas analysis or a dissolved oxygen probe.
  Priority: 6.

EXPERIMENT 1.3 - Non growth ATP maintenance
  What to do: Run a chemostat at three or more dilution rates. Plot specific ATP demand
    against growth rate. The intercept is the non growth maintenance value.
  Website match: the ATP maintenance floor in the FBA network (constant atpMaintenance).
  Correlation: Weak to Moderate. It trims the achievable yields slightly.
  Feasibility: Hard. Needs a chemostat and steady state sampling at several conditions.
  Priority: 3.

EXPERIMENT 1.4 - Precursor flux to intracellular concentration
  What to do: Quench and extract cells, then use LC-MS to measure the intracellular
    L-glutamate pool. Regress the measured concentration against the precursor flux the
    FBA predicts for the same condition.
  Website match: the "Precursor to ODE concentration" readout that couples the FBA to the
    kinetics model (constant fluxToConc).
  Correlation: Strong. This is the single conversion that hands the FBA result to the
    cross linking and metabolic modules, so an error here propagates everywhere the
    polymer appears.
  Feasibility: Hard. Needs rapid quenching and LC-MS access.
  Priority: 9.

VALIDATION (no constant, but a headline prediction to test)
  The FBA predicts that deleting the acetate overflow genes reroutes carbon toward
  gamma-PGA. Build the knockout strain and compare acetate excretion and polymer yield to
  wild type. This confirms the knockout panel in the module is telling the truth.

MODULE 2 - CENTRAL DOGMA AND KINETICS  (website: "From gene to glue")
===========================================================================
This module tracks message, enzyme, and polymer over time. Its rates are currently
literature values for Bacillus subtilis.

EXPERIMENT 2.1 - Messenger RNA level and half life
  What to do: Use quantitative PCR or RNA sequencing on the synthase transcript. For half
    life, block transcription with rifampicin and follow the transcript decay.
  Website match: the transcription rate and message degradation rate in the kinetics ODE.
  Correlation: Moderate. Sets how fast the enzyme pool builds and how quickly it responds.
  Feasibility: Moderate. Standard molecular biology once primers are designed.
  Priority: 5.

EXPERIMENT 2.2 - Enzyme turnover number
  What to do: Purify or partially purify the synthase and the polymer degrading enzyme,
    then run activity assays across substrate concentrations to fit the turnover number.
  Website match: the catalytic rate that converts glutamate into polymer, and the polymer
    cut back rate.
  Correlation: Strong for the polymer accumulation curve.
  Feasibility: Hard. Needs protein purification and an activity assay.
  Priority: 5.

VALIDATION
  The module claims that knocking out the polymer degrading enzymes lets gamma-PGA pile
  up. Compare polymer titre in the degradation knockout strain against wild type to
  confirm the accumulation the animation shows.

MODULE 3 - CALCIUM CROSS-LINKING  (website: "Goo into gel")
===========================================================================
Turns the polymer plus calcium into a stiffness value. The binding curve and the network
density are the calibration targets.

EXPERIMENT 3.1 - Calcium binding constant of gamma-PGA
  What to do: Titrate calcium chloride into a gamma-PGA solution and fit the binding
    curve. Isothermal titration calorimetry is the clean method. A cheaper route is a
    calcium ion selective electrode titration.
  Website match: the calcium dissociation constant in the cross linking module
    (constant KdPGA). Also sets the polymer affinity in the shared calcium competition.
  Correlation: Strong. It sets the calcium level at which the gel actually stiffens.
  Feasibility: Moderate with an ion selective electrode, Hard if using calorimetry.
  Priority: 7.

EXPERIMENT 3.2 - Broth yield to in pore network density
  What to do: Treat a known volume of sand with broth of measured polymer yield. Rinse,
    dry, and weigh the retained polymer per unit volume.
  Website match: the mapping from broth yield to network density (constant yieldToRho).
  Correlation: Moderate. Scales the stiffness output.
  Feasibility: Easy. Gravimetric.
  Priority: 6.

EXPERIMENT 3.3 - Pore saturation ceiling
  What to do: Column tests. Increase polymer loading until permeability collapses or no
    further strength is gained. That plateau is the ceiling.
  Website match: the saturation cap on network density (constant rhoCap).
  Correlation: Weak. Only matters at high loading.
  Feasibility: Moderate.
  Priority: 3.

EXPERIMENT 3.4 - Polymer molecular weight
  What to do: Gel permeation chromatography with multi angle light scattering on the
    secreted polymer.
  Website match: the molecular weight inputs in the module (Mx and Mn).
  Correlation: Weak. Adjusts the finite chain correction.
  Feasibility: Hard. Needs the chromatography instrument.
  Priority: 3.

MODULE 4 - CALCIUM CARBONATE PRECIPITATION  (website: "Turning carbon dioxide into rock")
===========================================================================
Geochemistry that turns enzyme activity into cemented strength. This module carries the
headline biocement claim.

EXPERIMENT 4.1 - Carbonic anhydrase activity enhancement
  What to do: Run an esterase assay on the displayed enzyme cells against an empty vector
    control, or a do it yourself phenol red pH drop assay on washed whole cells bubbled
    with carbon dioxide (watch the red to yellow rate). The activity ratio of displayed
    enzyme to control sets the realized enhancement.
  Website match: the rate enhancement fed to the precipitation model
    (constant caRateEnhancement, feeding the carbonic anhydrase activity fraction).
  Correlation: Strong. It sets how much faster carbonate forms with the engineered enzyme.
  Feasibility: Easy for the phenol red version, Moderate for the esterase assay.
  Priority: 9.

EXPERIMENT 4.2 - Cemented strength versus calcite content
  What to do: Make cemented sand cores at several calcite contents. Run an unconfined
    compression test on each. Measure calcite content by acid digestion or thermal
    gravimetric analysis. Fit strength against calcite content on a log log plot to get
    the slope and intercept.
  Website match: the strength law in the module (constants kUcs and nUcs).
  Correlation: Strong. This is the headline biocement strength curve.
  Feasibility: Moderate to Hard. Needs a compression rig and a calcite content assay.
  Priority: 9.

EXPERIMENT 4.3 - Surface precipitation rate
  What to do: In a stirred cell, mix calcium and carbonate at a known supersaturation and
    track calcium depletion over time with an ion selective electrode. Fit rate against
    supersaturation.
  Website match: the precipitation rate constant (constant kPrecip).
  Correlation: Moderate. Sets how fast the crust forms, not its final strength.
  Feasibility: Moderate.
  Priority: 6.

EXPERIMENT 4.4 - Amorphous to calcite ripening rate
  What to do: Use time resolved X-ray diffraction or Raman to follow the fraction of
    crystalline calcite versus amorphous material over time. Fit a first order rate. Note
    that field results in the region often yield vaterite as well, so record which
    polymorph appears.
  Website match: the ripening rate that makes the crust harden with age
    (constant kAccToCalcite), and the vaterite branch of the model.
  Correlation: Moderate. Drives the aging and curing behaviour.
  Feasibility: Hard. Needs diffraction or Raman time series.
  Priority: 6.

EXPERIMENT 4.5 - Solubility and acid constants
  What to do: Use literature values but verify the ionic strength of actual site pore
    water, and temperature correct the acid constants to desert surface temperature.
  Website match: the solubility products and acid dissociation constants in the module.
  Correlation: Weak. Shifts the saturation threshold slightly.
  Feasibility: Easy (calculation plus a water analysis).
  Priority: 3.

MODULE 5 - SURFACE DISPLAY AND ANCHORING  (website: "Bolting an enzyme outside")
===========================================================================
This module compares two ways to display the enzyme on the cell surface and estimates the
active surface fraction from a chain of efficiencies.

EXPERIMENT 5.1 - Surface display proof of concept
  What to do: Three planned assays feed this directly. First, retention of a reporter
    protein on coated beads to show the anchoring works. Second, the whole cell activity
    assay from Experiment 4.1 to show displayed enzyme is active. Third, a periplasmic
    fractionation to confirm the export signal peptide is working.
  Website match: the per step success fractions that multiply into the active surface
    fraction in the anchoring module.
  Correlation: Moderate. Sets the realistic ceiling on displayed activity.
  Feasibility: Moderate. All three are standard once constructs are in hand.
  Priority: 6.

MODULE 6 - ALGINATE GEL  (website: "The egg-box gel")
===========================================================================
Third prong gel. Its junction density and its washout are the calibration targets.

EXPERIMENT 6.1 - Guluronate fraction
  What to do: Read it from the supplier certificate of analysis, or measure it by proton
    NMR block analysis on the exact lot used.
  Website match: the guluronate fraction that sets junction density (constant
    guluronateFraction).
  Correlation: Moderate. Scales gel stiffness.
  Feasibility: Easy from the certificate, Moderate by NMR.
  Priority: 6.

EXPERIMENT 6.2 - Washout per wet cycle
  What to do: Rainfall simulation. Subject treated coupons to repeated wetting and measure
    residual alginate or residual strength against cycle number. Fit the fractional loss
    per cycle.
  Website match: the washout rate per cycle used in the durability and resilience scoring
    (constant washoutRatePerCycle).
  Correlation: Strong for the rain and flood resilience story.
  Feasibility: Easy to Moderate.
  Priority: 6.

EXPERIMENT 6.3 - Calcium binding constant of alginate
  What to do: Calcium ion selective electrode titration of the alginate solution.
  Website match: the alginate calcium affinity (constant KdCa), also used in the shared
    calcium competition.
  Correlation: Moderate.
  Feasibility: Moderate.
  Priority: 4.

EXPERIMENT 6.4 - Applied concentration to network density and water holding
  What to do: Gravimetric. Measure retained alginate per treated sand volume against
    applied concentration, and water uptake at controlled humidity using saturated salt
    chambers.
  Website match: the concentration to density mapping and the water holding capacity
    (constants concToRho and waterHoldingCapacity).
  Correlation: Weak to Moderate.
  Feasibility: Easy.
  Priority: 3.

MODULE 7 - PROTEIN THERMAL STABILITY  (website: "Heat switches it off")
===========================================================================
This module gates every enzyme rate by the folded fraction at a given temperature.

EXPERIMENT 7.1 - Melting temperature of each enzyme
  What to do: Differential scanning fluorimetry (thermal shift assay) or differential
    scanning calorimetry on the purified carbonic anhydrase and the synthase. Read the
    melting temperature and the unfolding enthalpy from the transition.
  Website match: the melting point and curve steepness in the thermal module.
  Correlation: Strong. The whole toolkit is gated by the folded fraction, so an accurate
    melting temperature is what makes the desert temperature claim honest.
  Feasibility: Moderate. Thermal shift assays are quick if a real time PCR machine is
    available.
  Priority: 7.

MODULE 8 - GROWTH AND KILL SWITCH  (website: "Spreading safely")
===========================================================================
Growth plus containment. The spread model uses a growth rate and a diffusion term, and
the kill switch defines the boundary.

EXPERIMENT 8.1 - Growth rate on sand relevant medium
  What to do: Standard growth curves (OD600 over time) in the deployment relevant medium
    to get the maximum growth rate.
  Website match: the growth rate that drives the spread front.
  Correlation: Moderate.
  Feasibility: Easy.
  Priority: 5.

EXPERIMENT 8.2 - Kill switch characterization
  What to do: Challenge the engineered strain with the trigger condition and measure
    survival, plus the leak rate under the permissive condition.
  Website match: the containment boundary in the ecological module.
  Correlation: Strong for the biosafety claim.
  Feasibility: Moderate. Needs the built kill switch strain.
  Priority: 6.

MODULE 9 - AEOLIAN WIND EROSION  (website: "Stopping the sand" and the wind tunnel)
===========================================================================
This is where the wind tunnel earns its place. It converts crust strength into the wind
speed the surface can resist. Two bridge constants here are the most important in the
whole toolkit because they turn every prong strength into the final wind claim.

EXPERIMENT 9.1 - Shear modulus to cohesion bridge (polymer)
  What to do: On the same treated coupons, pair a rheometer or dynamic mechanical modulus
    with a cohesion measurement (micro penetrometer or torsional shear). The slope links
    modulus to cohesion.
  Website match: the polymer strength to wind cohesion bridge (constant cohesionPerG).
  Correlation: Strong. This converts the cross linking output into wind resistance.
  Feasibility: Hard. Needs both a modulus measurement and a cohesion measurement on the
    same samples.
  Priority: 9.

EXPERIMENT 9.2 - Cemented strength to cohesion bridge (calcite)
  What to do: Pair the unconfined compression strength from Experiment 4.2 with the
    cohesion intercept from a direct shear box on cemented cores. The slope is the bridge.
  Website match: the cement strength to wind cohesion bridge (constant cohesionPerUCS).
  Correlation: Strong. Same role for the calcite prong.
  Feasibility: Hard. Needs a direct shear box.
  Priority: 9.

EXPERIMENT 9.3 - Bagnold threshold parameter
  What to do: In the wind tunnel, on untreated sieved sand, ramp the wind and record the
    first sustained grain motion against grain size using high speed video or a saltation
    sensor. Fit the threshold parameter.
  Website match: the Bagnold threshold parameter (constant A).
  Correlation: Moderate. Sets the bare sand baseline the crust is compared against.
  Feasibility: Moderate to Hard. Needs the wind tunnel.
  Priority: 6.

EXPERIMENT 9.4 - Wind profile coupling
  What to do: Measure the vertical velocity profile in the tunnel with a pitot rake or hot
    wire and fit the log law to get friction velocity against free stream speed.
  Website match: the friction velocity to free stream coupling (constant uStarRatio).
  Correlation: Moderate.
  Feasibility: Moderate.
  Priority: 6.

EXPERIMENT 9.5 - Saltation flux coefficient
  What to do: Use a downwind sand trap. Plot mass flux against the cube of friction
    velocity. The slope gives the coefficient.
  Website match: the saltation mass flux coefficient (constant saltationC).
  Correlation: Weak. Affects transport magnitude above threshold, not the threshold.
  Feasibility: Moderate.
  Priority: 3.

MODULE 10 - WET LAB SANDBOX  (website: "Bench to dune")
===========================================================================
This module is a front end. It does not add its own constants. It takes bench inputs
(culture density, glutamate feed, salinity) and runs them through the same erosion model
as Module 9. Calibrating Modules 1, 3, 4, and 9 automatically calibrates this one. No
separate experiment is required. The correct action is to make sure the bench inputs it
exposes match the actual variables the team controls at the bench.

MODULE 11 - GRAIN-SIZE COVERAGE  (website: "No one glue fits all")
===========================================================================
Coverage is integrated over the site grain size distribution, so the site sand itself is
a calibration input.

EXPERIMENT 11.1 - Site grain size distribution
  What to do: Sieve stack or laser diffraction on the actual deployment site sand. Read
    the median grain size and the spread.
  Website match: the site distribution inputs (constants uaeD50 and uaeSizeSigma).
  Correlation: Strong. The whole coverage integral is taken over this distribution.
  Feasibility: Easy with a sieve stack.
  Priority: 7.

EXPERIMENT 11.2 - Cement sweet spot versus grain size
  What to do: Make cemented cores in narrow sieved size bands and run unconfined
    compression on each. Locate the peak strength grain size and the fall off on each
    side.
  Website match: the cement coverage peak and width (constants micpPeakDiameter and
    micpLogWidth).
  Correlation: Moderate. Sets where the cement prong is strong and where the other two
    have to cover.
  Feasibility: Moderate.
  Priority: 6.

EXPERIMENT 11.3 - Polymer and alginate coverage bands
  What to do: Wet sieve aggregate stability or cohesion tests on polymer only and alginate
    only treated bands across the size range.
  Website match: the polymer and alginate coverage curves in the module.
  Correlation: Weak to Moderate. Refines the overlap picture.
  Feasibility: Moderate.
  Priority: 4.

MODULE 12 - CURING TIMELINE  (website: "Set, age, renew")
===========================================================================
Each binder sets on its own clock. The set times and the calcite aging drive this module.

EXPERIMENT 12.1 - Time resolved strength of each binder
  What to do: Treat coupons and measure strength or gel state at intervals across the
    curing protocol. Alginate sets in minutes, polymer over hours, calcite over the full
    protocol window. The calcite ripening rate comes from Experiment 4.4.
  Website match: the per binder set times and the calcite aging floor in the curing module.
  Correlation: Moderate. Sets the shape of the strength versus time curve and the
    reapplication interval.
  Feasibility: Moderate.
  Priority: 5.

MODULE 13 - COMPOSITE STRENGTH AND INTERACTIONS  (website: "Why three beat one")
===========================================================================
The composite total adds synergy and subtracts competition on top of the single prong
strengths. These require paired combination cores.

EXPERIMENT 13.1 - Pairwise synergy coefficients
  What to do: Make single prong cores, two prong cores, and the three prong core. Run
    direct shear or unconfined compression on each. The synergy of a pair is the combined
    strength minus the two single strengths, divided by the square root of their product.
  Website match: the three synergy coefficients in the composite panel.
  Correlation: Moderate. Sets how much the combination beats the sum of parts.
  Feasibility: Moderate. Many cores but standard tests.
  Priority: 6.

EXPERIMENT 13.2 - Shared calcium pool and per prong demand
  What to do: Titrate available calcium in treated site soil with an ion selective
    electrode, and measure calcium bound per gram of each binder. For calcite, measure
    calcium consumed per unit calcite formed.
  Website match: the shared calcium supply and per prong demand in the interaction model.
  Correlation: Moderate. Drives the competition penalty between prongs.
  Feasibility: Moderate.
  Priority: 6.

EXPERIMENT 13.3 - Co-expression burden
  What to do: Compare polymer and enzyme titres in single function strains against the
    dual function strain. The ratio is the retained titre fraction.
  Website match: the co-expression burden factor in the interaction model.
  Correlation: Moderate.
  Feasibility: Moderate. Needs the dual function strain.
  Priority: 6.

EXPERIMENT 13.4 - Resilience stress assays
  What to do: Refine the per scenario robustness scores with targeted stress tests.
    Rainfall simulation for flood, culture kill followed by residual strength for
    bacterial death, bake and retest for drought and heat, wind tunnel survival time for
    high wind, and accelerated weathering for long term durability.
  Website match: the resilience radar scores in the composite panel.
  Correlation: Moderate for the robustness narrative.
  Feasibility: Moderate.
  Priority: 5.

MODULE 14 - COST AND SCALABILITY  (website: "Does it pencil out?")
===========================================================================
This module builds cost from the ground up. It is fed by production numbers, not by a
standalone experiment.

FEED 14.1 - Production titres and yields
  What to do: Report the fermentation titre and yield for the polymer and the enzyme from
    the runs in Modules 1 and 2, and the purchased alginate cost.
  Website match: the per prong cost inputs in the economic module.
  Correlation: Strong for the crossover point against conventional methods.
  Feasibility: Easy once fermentation runs exist.
  Priority: 5.

===========================================================================

    quantitative FBA module.
2.  Carbonic anhydrase activity assay (Experiment 4.1). Unlocks the precipitation realism
    and the anchoring decision.

    strength curve.
4.  Cohesion bridges (Experiments 9.1 and 9.2). Connect every prong strength to wind
    resistance.
5.  Wind tunnel threshold and profile (Experiments 9.3 and 9.4). Anchor the erosion claim.
6.  Site grain size distribution and cement band strength (Experiments 11.1 and 11.2).
    Ground the coverage story in the real site sand.
7.  Melting temperature (Experiment 7.1). Make the desert temperature gate honest.
8.  Paired combination cores and calcium competition (Experiments 13.1 to 13.3). Validate
    the composite synergy and competition story.

Everything else is refinement to run once the above are in.
