Kill Switch
Problem 1: Prevent the overspread of engineered Bacillus Subtilis (BS).
Plasmid vs Chromosome? Chromosome
MazF (on chromosome) = Toxin (the killer); Already present on BS chromosome
MazE (on chromosome)= Antitoxin (the protector); Already present in BS chromosome
● MazE + MazF together = safe (antitoxin cancels toxin)
aTc trigger = The "kill signal"
This requires the following promoters on the chromosome:
[Constitutive promoter] ──→ mazE (antitoxin - always ON); Already in BS
[Constitutive promoter] ──→ mazF (toxin - always ON); Already in BS
[Tet-inducible promoter] ──→ mazF (toxin - OFF until aTc added); Not already in BS
Add aTc → MazF gene turns ON → too much MazF for MazE to handle → cell dies
Now we can kill BS even before the 20 generations timer! YAYY!

Kill Switch
Problem 2: Prevent engineered genes from our BS from passing to other wild bacteria in the desert.
Chromosome vs Plasmid? Plasmid
On the plasmid → add E Coli. Maz F gene (toxin)
On the chromosome → add E Coli. Maz E gene (anti-toxin)
HGT happens in one of two way:
- Only Plasmid Transfer: Wild bacteria dies
- Plasmid Transfer + Chromosome Fragmentation: Only one case where this could be problem: bacteria picks up E
Coli Maz E from chromosome. To prevent this, we could do: Maz E Splitting, Codon Optimization
But… can’t the wild BS just make it own anti-toxin to neutralize the toxin acquired by HGT?
No. MazE and MazF must come from the SAME organism to neutralize each other.
What happens after 20 generations/ genes on plasmid are lost?
Kill Switch stops working. However, by this time the plasmid has lost the biofilm genes we added as well. So there’s no
use for the kill switch anymore and therefore, we don’t mind if kill switch stops working now!
